5 warning signs a message was written by AI

Scammers now write perfect English and can copy a loved one's voice from three seconds of audio. The old advice — "look for bad spelling" — is dead. Here's the new advice, in plain English.

⏰
1. It creates urgency "Act now", "within 24 hours", "before your account is closed", "don't tell anyone". Real organisations — and real family members — almost never need you to act this second. Urgency is the scammer's most important tool, because it stops you thinking. What to do: slow down. Anything genuinely urgent will survive a ten-minute pause.
💷
2. It ends in money or codes However the story starts — a parcel, a grandchild in trouble, a tax refund, a security alert — it always ends the same way: move money, buy vouchers, or read out a code from a text. What to do: the moment money or a code is mentioned, treat the whole conversation as suspicious.
📞
3. The voice is right but something's off AI voice clones sound exactly like your loved one — but they avoid open questions, push you to act fast, and the call quality often "conveniently" drops. What to do: hang up and call the person back on the number you already have for them. A real relative won't mind. Better still, agree a family safe word in advance.
✨
4. It's a bit too polished Flawless grammar, oddly formal phrasing, your name used slightly too often, and details that are generic when you look twice ("your recent order", "your bank"). What to do: ask yourself — does this person normally write like this?
🔗
5. It wants you to click, not check The message provides its own link, its own phone number, its own "verify here" button — anything to stop you going to the real website or real person yourself. What to do: never use the contact route the message gives you. Go to the organisation directly, using the address or number you find yourself.

The golden rule: scammers need speed and secrecy. Your defence is slowness and a second opinion.
